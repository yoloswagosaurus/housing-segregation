Housing Segregation

The Story of Housing Segregation and Redlining, Specifically in Portland, OR

What is Housing Segregation?

Housing Segregation is a major race-targeted issue across many cities in the United States. Our research started as a search for what housing segregation truly is, and the places it comes from. Housing segregation is a form of discrimination (primarily racial discrimination) that separates and splits up groups of people into separate neighborhoods and housing situations. For example, in a city, the White citizens may live in more convenient upkept neighborhoods, while the Black community may live in a neighborhood with degrading houses and few functional commodities. From this came our research question:

To what extent has the fight against housing segregation and redlining in Portland contributed to the makeup of modern neighborhoods?

Our Thesis

We split our research into three separate questions: To what degree has progress against housing segregation been challenged, how has a history of housing segregation in Portland contributed to modern policy in law, and compared to other U.S. cities, how much has Portland done to improve housing segregation? We individually went off to figure out the answers to our questions. Upon finishing our research we ended up with our thesis.

Thesis Statement: The fight against redlining and housing segregation has profoundly contributed to the makeup of Portland’s modern communities, as activists have overcome tough obstacles in order to ensure justice for all.

1948, in the case Shelley v. Kraemer the Supreme Court ruled that it should no longer be legal to discriminate against people of different races, ethnicities, religions, or genders while selling a home. This was one of the biggest steps toward equitable housing at the beginning of the civil rights movement and helped many people obtain homes from a salesperson who was required by law to be equitable. Another key factor in the fight against housing segregation was the 1964 Civil Rights Act passed under the Johnson administration, which ended all segregation in any public place including real estate brokerages.

Compared to other cities, Portland's history with housing segregation has been very complicated. The Albina part of Portland (now integrated into the east side) specifically was an impoverished area with failing structures and was almost entirely composed of non-White people. However, activism was sparked fairly early in Portland, both from inside and outside the poor neighborhoods, and this quickly started a pushback against racism. Portland still feels the impact of housing segregation, and likely always will, but we’re on the rise. Cities on the east side of the United States continue to feel the heavy effect of this discrimination, and compared to Portland are doing much worse. In a way, Portland has made an impressive comeback from a dark part of history.

Learn More About Housing Segregation

The fight against housing segregation is an ongoing struggle that continues to shape communities across the United States. Learning more about this critical issue can inspire activists to make a difference all over the country. Exploration of the history of housing segregation in Portland can lead to a better understanding of its causes and effects, and can help us better understand our world.
